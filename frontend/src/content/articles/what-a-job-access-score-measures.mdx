The signature metric on MetroTrack is a **job-access score**: for a given place,
how many jobs can a rider reach within a fixed travel-time budget using transit
and walking. It compresses a lot of geography into one comparable number, which
is exactly why it's worth being precise about what it does and doesn't say.

## The ingredients

A job-access score is a spatial join of three layers:

1. **Where jobs are** — employment counts by small geographic area.
2. **Where people can get to** — travel-time areas (isochrones) computed from
   transit schedules plus a short walk at each end.
3. **The budget** — a fixed cutoff, commonly 45 minutes, that defines "reachable."

Multiply reachable areas by the jobs inside them, sum, and you have the score.
Change the budget and the number moves — a 30-minute score and a 60-minute score
answer different questions.

## What it captures well

- **Network effect, not just proximity.** Living near a rail line only helps if
  that line goes where the jobs are.
- **Comparisons across places** under identical assumptions.
- **Change over time** as service is added or cut.

## What it deliberately omits

A single number hides real texture, and honesty means naming the gaps:

- It counts *jobs reachable*, not *jobs you'd want or qualify for*.
- Schedule-based travel times assume the trip goes as timetabled; real-world
  reliability is a separate measurement.
- Off-peak and late-night access can differ sharply from the peak figure.

> A good score tells you the door is open. It doesn't promise the trip is pleasant.

When this metric ships, every score on the site will show its travel-time budget,
the schedule date it was computed from, and a link to the underlying method — so
the number is legible, not magic.
